
Dear Sir or Madam,

As a student at our university, **I am writing to request that** internet access be installed in the students' dormitories.

First and foremost, professors increasingly require us to use the internet for research, and much of the communication between professors and students **is conducted via email**. Additionally, some professors are now posting lecture notes online for students to review **at their convenience**.

While there are a few computer labs with internet access on campus, **the demand far exceeds availability**, given that over 20,000 students reside in the dormitories. This highlights ==a pressing need== for more accessible internet options.

Thank you for your time and consideration. Your prompt attention to this request would be greatly appreciated.

Yours sincerely,  
Li Ming

### **subjunctive mood**

In English, when using verbs like *request*, *demand*, *suggest*, or *recommend*, we often follow them with a structure called the **subjunctive mood**. In this structure, *should* is usually omitted because the verb that follows stays in its base form (infinitive without *to*), indicating a recommendation, request, or necessity rather than a direct statement of fact. 

Here’s how it works:

- **Request that + [subject] + [base form of the verb]**
- Example: "I request that he **go** to the meeting."

So, in your sentence:

- *I am writing to request that internet access **be** installed in the students' dormitories.*

Using *should* is not incorrect, but it makes the sentence less formal. The subjunctive form *be* is often preferred in formal writing because it conveys the request more directly.

Here are both versions for comparison:

1. **Subjunctive (formal):** "I am writing to request that internet access **be** installed..."
2. **With *should* (less formal):** "I am writing to request that internet access **should be** installed..."

The subjunctive form *be* is simply more concise and commonly used in formal requests like this one.
